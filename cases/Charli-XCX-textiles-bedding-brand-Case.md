## Phase 1: Deep Profile

**Celebrity:** Charli XCX (Charlotte Emma Aitchison)
**Public Persona:** Hyperpop pioneer, Brat-era cultural architect, illegal rave veteran, meta-performer who deconstructs pop stardom while embodying it. Two homes — a minimal Hackney flat (Ligne Roset Togo, stacks of books, white walls) and a maximalist LA compound (black-on-black kitchen, glass walls, 18ft beamed ceiling). Lives in the friction between underground credibility and mainstream luxury.

**Core Values:**
1. **Authenticity Through Fantasy** — Rejects the demand for literal honesty from artists. "I don't care if they tell the truth or lie or play a character or adopt a persona or fabricate entire scenarios and worlds. To me that's the point, that's the drama, that's the fun, that's the FANTASY." Authenticity for her is emotional, not factual.
2. **Vulnerability as Confrontation** — Her most aggressive work is also her most vulnerable. She described *Brat* as "heavy, confusing and a very vulnerable change." At Sundance 2026, she called herself "quite a volatile person." She treats openness not as softness but as a provocation.

**Aesthetic DNA:** Berlin Raw × Club Culture Hybrid
- London illegal rave scene roots → raw textures, sweat, strobe light, concrete
- Hyperpop / digital maximalism → glitched, compressed, distorted surfaces
- Brat green (Pantone 3570 C) → Y2K acid-lime, unapologetic, trashy-on-purpose
- High fashion × gutter energy → Acne Studios + Saint Laurent campaigns, but always subverted
- Meta-performance → *The Moment* (A24, Feb 2026) satirizes her own fame machine

**Product Category:** Textiles / Bedding — the morning-after energy, the domestic crash, the body at rest after the body in motion

## Phase 1.5: Trend Brief

### Category Pulse
- **Texture-maxxing leads 2026 bedding** — "Natural nonchalance" is the design-forward look: garment-washed cotton, enzyme finishes, deliberately imperfect textures that feel broken-in from day one. Shift away from crisp, hotel-like perfection toward surfaces that have *lived*.
- **Dopamine decor fatigue** — The bright, playful bedding of 2023–2024 gives way to grounding tones: warm taupe, mineral blue, forest green, clay. Consumers want bedding that absorbs rather than broadcasts.
- **Artisanal techniques at luxury scale** — Handwoven, tufted, quilted, and block-printed textiles crossing from niche to international market. But no brand has claimed the *subculture* angle — bedding for the body that partied, not the body that meditated.

### Celebrity Current Moment
- **"The Moment" (A24, Feb 2026)** — Mockumentary satirizing Brat-era fame. Charli plays a heightened version of herself struggling between artistic integrity and commercial pressure. The film is meta-commentary on branding itself.
- **Saint Laurent S/S 2026 campaign** — Shot by Glen Luchford, art directed by Vaccarello. Grainy, louche, colorful anoraks meeting gorp-meets-coquette styling. Charli as the embodiment of "heritage glamour × contemporary audacity."
- **Wallpaper* Design Award** — Brat campaign recognized for culturally impactful visual design. Pantone 3570 C + blurred Arial = the most memeable album identity since *Unknown Pleasures*. She understands materiality at the level of a graphic designer.
- **"I want to quit music"** — Told Quen Blackwell she hopes to pursue acting over music. The transition itself is her current frequency — a body between states.

### Macro Winds
- **The "3-6-5 party girl" aesthetic exists in fashion but has NO home equivalent** — The rolled-from-bed-to-bar look is a recognized style direction, but no brand has extended this energy into domestic objects. The after-party has no interior design language.
- **Early-evening dance parties rise** — "Because we have shit to do in the morning." The relationship between nightlife and daytime productivity is being renegotiated. The crash isn't escapism — it's recovery infrastructure.
- **Sustainability meets texture** — Recycled cotton yarns becoming standard. Garment-dyed, enzyme-washed fabrics can now achieve the "dead soft" hand at scale without the environmental cost of traditional softening processes.

### Tension Worth Exploiting
The "after party" exists as a fashion aesthetic and a cultural mood, but no brand has claimed the *domestic* version of that energy — the bedding, the textiles, the surfaces your body crashes into at 4 AM. Charli XCX is the artist who turned party-girl chaos into a Wallpaper* Design Award-winning visual system. The gap: between "going-out fashion" and "coming-home objects."

## Phase 2: The "Edit" Scan

### Named Movement: Warm Static (余韻 — Yo'in)

### Voo Store Filter — Raw × Refined
Where is the tension? In the collision between club-sweat energy and the softness of bedding. Industrial textures (raw-edge selvedge, visible weave structure, garment-dye irregularity) meet the most intimate domestic surface — the sheet you press your face into. The product should feel like it was made in a Berlin textile atelier, not a factory: heavyweight construction, imperfect finishing, exposed thread ends. But the *hand* — the feel against skin — is pure luxury: enzyme-washed until all rigidity dissolves, a surface you could sleep on for twelve hours without a crease mark. The outside says *screen-printed in a Kreuzberg studio*. The inside says *engineered for a body that earned its rest*.

### TSUTAYA Filter — Lifestyle Proposal
This is not "bedding." It is a proposal for how a body *transitions*. The Static Set is organized not by thread count or color name but by *state*: "Frequency Grey" is the standard crash, "Dawn Static" is the lighter variant for the body that will wake early, "Club Black" is for the body that wants total absorption. Each set ships in a drawstring muslin bag that doubles as a laundry bag or travel roll — because the person who buys DEAD SOFT crashes in different places. The bag is the product. The sheet is the ritual.

### Indie Print Filter — Analog Appreciation
Where is the analog warmth? In the packaging typography: blurred Arial (the Brat typeface) screen-printed in acid green on raw muslin. In the care label: not a woven tag but a hand-stamped linen patch that says "MACHINE WASH HOT. THE HOTTER THE SOFTER." In the zine insert: an 8-page Riso-printed booklet that replaces the care card, telling the material story in the register of *Apartamento* — photographs of unmade beds, bodies at rest, morning light on crumpled fabric. The product ages with you. It is designed to look better after 50 washes, not worse.

### Visual Mood
A tangled flat sheet on an unmade bed, warm 4 AM light cutting through half-drawn blinds at a low angle. The fabric is heavyweight washed cotton — not pristine white but a warm, worn grey with a faint ghost of acid green visible only at the selvedge edge. One pillow bears the indent of a head that just left. A phone charges face-down on the floor beside a half-drunk glass of water. The surface is *Apartamento* at its most intimate: the beauty of a room that was lived in hard and is now at rest. Shot on medium-format film — visible grain, slightly warm color temperature, the kind of photograph that smells like cotton and skin and the last note of a perfume worn hours ago.

## Phase 3: The Blueprint

### 1. Brand Name & Core Logic

**DEAD SOFT** (デッドソフト)

*Core Statement:* "Your body has a frequency. This is what it sounds like at rest."

In metallurgy, "dead soft" describes metal that has been fully annealed — heated to extreme temperatures and slowly cooled until all internal stress is released, reaching maximum pliability. Zero hardness. Maximum malleability.

Charli XCX is an artist who has been through the fire: illegal raves at 16, hyperpop's compressed intensity, a Brat era that became a Wallpaper* Design Award-winning cultural event, and an A24 film deconstructing her own fame. The brand proposes that what comes *after* the performance — the crash, the horizontal surrender, the 4 AM stillness — deserves its own material world. DEAD SOFT is bedding and textiles for the body in its post-performance state: maximum softness earned through maximum intensity. The product is not for "sleep." It is for the specific relief of a body that was ON and is now OFF.

### 2. Hero Product

**The Static Set** — 3-piece bedding system: fitted sheet, flat sheet, pillowcase pair.

**Material:** 400GSM organic cotton percale, garment-dyed in small batches and enzyme-washed three times to achieve the "dead soft" hand — zero stiffness, maximum drape, the tactile equivalent of a t-shirt that has been through 200 wash cycles. The fabric has a matte, almost chalky surface quality. No sheen. No polish. The weave is dense enough to feel substantial against the face but breathable enough for a body that is still cooling down from six hours of movement.

**Color:** "Frequency Grey" — a warm, worn stone grey with a single acid-green thread (Pantone 3570 C) running through the selvedge edge. The green is almost invisible when the sheet is on the bed. You only see it when you fold it, pull back the corner, or get close enough to touch. It is a private signature — the residue of a former life as a neon-green Brat-era artifact, now washed into something quieter. Two additional colorways: "Dawn Static" (pale ash, almost white, with the same green thread) and "Club Black" (deep carbon black, the green thread barely visible even at the edge).

**Packaging:** The set ships in a screen-printed muslin drawstring bag — not a box. The bag doubles as a laundry bag or travel roll. Typography: blurred Arial in acid green on raw muslin, fading slightly at the edges as if photocopied one too many times. Inside: an 8-page Riso-printed zine replaces the traditional care card — material story, washing philosophy ("Machine wash hot. The hotter the softer."), and one photograph of an unmade bed shot by someone who understands that a crumpled sheet is more beautiful than a crisp one.

**Tactile Signature:** Pick up the pillowcase and it drapes over your hand like a second skin — there is weight but zero rigidity. The enzyme wash has broken every fiber until the fabric moves like liquid. Run your thumb along the selvedge edge and you feel the single green thread — a ridge of color in a field of grey.

### 3. Core Ritual / Scenario

**[ The 4 AM Horizontal ]**

You have been vertical for six hours — or eight, or twelve. Dancing, performing, working, being ON in whatever way your life demands. Your ears are still ringing. Your skin is cooling. The phone is on 4%. You don't brush your teeth. You don't set an alarm.

You peel off whatever you wore and fall — not lie, *fall* — onto a surface that absorbs you. Not into "luxury sleep" but into the specific relief of a body that earned its rest. The Static Set is designed for this threshold moment: heavyweight enough to feel like being held, soft enough to feel like dissolving. The flat sheet settles over you with the weight of permission.

The product does not optimize sleep. It does not track REM cycles. It does not promise "8 hours of restorative rest." It serves one moment: the crash. Everything after is your business.

- **Time:** 4:00 AM — or whenever vertical becomes impossible
- **Sound:** Whatever is still playing on the phone on the nightstand, volume at 3
- **Duration:** Until the light changes

### 4. Market Gap

**Position A: The Heirloom Set** — Frette, Matouk, Sferra. Beautiful linens sold through a language of heritage, thread count, and aspirational domesticity. They assume the bed is a sanctuary for gentle people with gentle lives. The photography shows a bed that no one has ever slept in.

**Position C: The Volume Comfort Set** — IKEA, H&M Home, Target Casaluna. Commodity comfort at scale. Adequate material, zero cultural address, no point of view. The bedding equivalent of fast fashion — it does the job and says nothing.

**Position B (the gap): DEAD SOFT** — No bedding brand speaks to the body that lives hard and crashes harder. No brand addresses the clubber, the performer, the nightshift creative, the touring musician — the person whose relationship with their bed is one of urgent, grateful collapse rather than curated relaxation. There is no Acne Studios of bedding, no *032c* of textiles. The "after party" aesthetic exists in fashion (the 3-6-5 party girl look, the rolled-from-bed-to-bar energy) but has zero domestic object equivalent.

**Why Charli XCX:** She literally lives this cycle. London warehouse raves at 16, global tours at 32, crashing in a Hackney flat with a Togo couch and stacks of books. She won a Wallpaper* Design Award for Brat's visual identity — proving she thinks about materiality at the level of a designer, not just a celebrity endorser. Her definition of her own aesthetic — "a pack of cigs, a Bic lighter, a strappy white top with no bra" — is a textile-first description of identity. Her two homes (minimal Hackney vs. maximalist LA) embody the exact tension DEAD SOFT resolves: a life of excess that recovers in restraint.

### 5. Magazine Spread

**Magazine:** *032c* (Berlin avant-garde, fashion-meets-theory)

**Left page:** Full-bleed photograph shot from directly above an unmade bed. The Static Set in Frequency Grey is tangled, one corner of the flat sheet dragging off the edge of a low platform bed onto raw concrete floor. A single pillow bears the deep indent of a head that left. Morning light — harsh, directional, low-angle — enters from the left, creating hard shadows in the fabric folds that read like topography. In the bottom-right corner of the bed, barely visible: a phone charging face-down, its cable the same acid green as the selvedge thread. The photograph has the grain of a Glen Luchford Saint Laurent campaign — not crisp, not aspirational, but intimate and slightly voyeuristic. Shot on medium-format film, printed with visible halftone dots.

**Right page:** White (actually warm grey) background, generous margin. Top: "DEAD SOFT" in blurred Arial — the Brat typeface shrunk to 8pt, barely legible, forcing the reader to lean in. Center: a single line of body text in DM Sans, left-aligned: *"Your body has a frequency. This is what it sounds like at rest."* Bottom third: product specifications in Space Mono, stacked left — material composition, garment-wash count, and a line in the same acid green as the selvedge: "MACHINE WASH HOT. THE HOTTER THE SOFTER." No celebrity photograph. No lifestyle shot. The right page is pure typography — the editorial confidence of *032c* at its most restrained.

### 6. Retail Space Concept

**Location:** A former industrial laundry in Kreuzberg, Berlin (the irony is the point).

**Environment:** Poured concrete floors with original drain channels still visible. Ceiling-mounted industrial strip lighting on dimmers — the space shifts from bright clinical (like a laundromat at noon) to warm amber (like a bedroom at 4 AM) over the course of each day. One wall: floor-to-ceiling raw steel shelving displaying the Static Set in all three colorways, each folded and wrapped in its muslin bag. The bags hang from butcher's hooks — industrial hanging that makes fabric look like equipment, not decoration.

Opposite wall: a single full-size bed, perfectly made each morning, then deliberately *unmade by hand* before the store opens. The bed is displayed in its crashed state — sheets pulled, pillow dented, one corner trailing to the floor. Visitors are invited to lie down. A small sign in Space Mono reads: "EVALUATE BY TOUCH. NO MIRRORS."

**Sound:** A continuous low-frequency ambient drone — the sonic equivalent of the hum in a room after the last guest has left. Designed to sit just below conscious awareness.

**Signature Detail:** The fitting room is a bed, not a changing room. Enclosed by floor-to-ceiling industrial curtains on a hospital track, each "fitting room" contains a single bed made with the Static Set. You pull the curtain, lie down, and decide if you're buying based entirely on how the fabric feels against your skin at rest. No visual evaluation. No mirrors. The product is assessed through the body's only honest surface: the face pressed into a pillowcase.

## Phase 4: Concept Board Prompt

### Reference Visual DNA

**Ref 1 — Soft-focus botanical still life (sage/olive monochrome):**
- Backlit, diffused, warm ambient glow — light through a curtain at dawn
- Monochromatic warm sage/olive, low saturation, yellowish-green cast
- Deliberately out of focus, dreamy haze — the world seen through half-open eyes
- 4 AM stillness, nostalgic, intimate

**Ref 2 — Fritz Hansen × Nonfiction field campaign:**
- Overcast natural daylight, golden hour foreground, misty background
- Warm earth tones — golden grass, olive foliage, desaturated greens and tans
- Design object placed alone in a natural field — environmental portrait, poetic solitude
- Contemplative, solitary, the object existing outside its expected context

**Ref 3 — Nonfiction "Young Summer Memories" fabric close-up:**
- Soft, even, diffused warm light
- Monochromatic golden-yellow, buttery warm tones
- Flowing textile with undulating folds as landscape-like topography, scattered white flowers
- Fabric IS the subject — surface as terrain, folds as geography

**Shared DNA:** Warm muted palette (sage, olive, gold — not bright green). Soft focus / atmospheric haze. Single objects in contemplative repose. Textile as terrain. Nonfiction register: organic, intimate, never aspirational.

### Image Generation Prompt

**Primary prompt (3:2 landscape — editorial spread):**

> A top-down editorial photograph of an unmade bed with crumpled heavyweight washed cotton bedsheets in a warm stone-grey tone. The fabric has a matte, chalky surface with visible weave texture — garment-washed percale with soft, undulating folds that create a terrain-like topography across the frame. A single thin acid-green thread runs along the selvedge edge of the flat sheet, barely visible, catching soft morning light. The sheets are tangled and pulled, one corner dragging off the bed edge. A single pillow bears the deep indent of a head. On the floor beside the bed: a phone face-down with a thin green charging cable, and a half-drunk glass of water. Warm, diffused early morning light enters from the left at a low angle, creating soft shadows in the fabric folds. The overall color palette is warm and muted — sage grey, warm stone, desaturated olive undertones — with the acid-green thread as the only color accent. Shot on medium-format film with visible grain, soft focus at the edges, slightly hazy atmosphere like looking through tired eyes. The mood is intimate, post-club, 4 AM stillness — Nonfiction × 032c editorial register. Analog warmth, tactile, the beauty of a room that was lived in hard and is now at rest. No people visible. The fabric itself is the subject.

**Negative prompt:** No bright colors, no crisp hotel-style bedding, no pristine white sheets, no sterile studio lighting, no digital sharpness, no lifestyle model, no decorative pillows, no staged styling, no cool blue tones.

**Secondary prompt (4:5 portrait — cover-ready):**

> Close-up top-down photograph of crumpled heavyweight washed cotton bedsheets in warm stone-grey, filling the entire frame. The fabric has a matte, chalky surface with visible garment-washed percale weave texture. Undulating folds create a landscape-like topography — valleys and ridges of soft fabric. A single thin acid-green thread (Pantone 3570 C) runs along the selvedge edge in the lower third of the frame, the only color accent in a field of warm grey. Scattered across the fabric surface: a few small dried flower petals (pale, almost white) that have fallen from somewhere unseen. Warm, diffused golden-hour light creates gentle shadows in the folds, giving the textile depth and dimension. The overall palette is warm and muted — stone grey with sage and olive undertones, golden light, desaturated. Shot on medium-format film with visible grain and a soft, dreamy haze quality. The fabric IS the subject — treated as terrain, as geography, as a surface that holds the residue of a body that was just here. Nonfiction × Apartamento aesthetic register. Intimate, tactile, analog.

**Negative prompt:** No bright neon green, no crisp flat-lay styling, no product packaging in frame, no digital sharpness, no cool tones, no commercial product photography feel.

**Art direction notes:**
- The refs share a Nonfiction (Korean brand) visual language — warm, organic, intimate, never aspirational
- Ref 1's soft-focus haze should inform the overall atmosphere (edges dissolving, not everything sharp)
- Ref 3's fabric-as-terrain approach is the compositional key — the sheets should look like landscape photography
- The acid-green selvedge thread should be subtle, not dominant — a discovery, not a statement
- Color grading should lean warm sage/olive (from ref 1) rather than cool grey
- The image should feel like it was found, not produced — an Apartamento photograph of someone's actual bed at 4 AM
