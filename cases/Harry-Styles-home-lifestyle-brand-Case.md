# Brand Blueprint: FONDRE

**Celebrity:** Harry Styles
**Product Category:** Home & Lifestyle (Candlery, Ceramics, Linens, Small Furniture)
**Curational Lens:** Global Indie (with Voo Store materiality bleed)
**Magazine Reference:** *Numéro*

---

## Phase 1: Deep Profile

**Aesthetic DNA:**
Harry Styles is the rare figure who has made fluidity itself an aesthetic position — not just gender fluidity in fashion (the Gucci gowns, the pearl necklaces, the painted nails), but a fluidity of *category*. He moves between pop and psychedelia, between stadium spectacle and intimate residency, between Chanel pinstripe and vintage market finds. His visual language is rooted in 1970s maximalism — saturated color, clashing pattern, furniture-scale jewelry — but filtered through a contemporary surrealism that makes the familiar feel slightly dreamed.

**Core Values:** Joy as subversion. Softness as strength. The domestic as creative canvas. His album *Harry's House* was not a metaphor — it was a declaration that the interior life (literally: interiors) is the artistic frontier.

**Persona Adjectives:** Gender-fluid. Maximalist. Surrealist.

## Phase 1.5: Trend Brief

### Category Pulse
- **Neo-surrealism dominates objects:** Maison & Objet 2025-2026 centered on surrealism as the design language of the moment — anatomical ceramics, melting forms, dreamlike materiality. The 2026 edition's theme "PAST REVEALS FUTURE" foregrounded four currents: Metamorphosis, Mutation, Revisited Baroque, Neo Folklore.
- **Artisan ceramics as cultural currency:** Tekla launched sculptural ceramic candle vessels with ceramicist Sara Flynn; Henry Holland Studio debuted hand-thrown ceramic vases in Berlin and London. Modularity and imperfection are the new premium signals.
- **Maximalism reclaims home decor:** 73% of designers surveyed prefer maximalism over minimalism for 2026 — expressed through fringe, fluid forms, whimsy, and "iron furniture that looks like draped fabric." Dezeen frames the mood as "curated calm over superficial opulence."

### Celebrity Current Moment
- **Pleasing pivots inward:** The Pleasing brand launched "Pleasing Yourself" (July 2025), a sexual wellness line, with a confessional-style pop-up in NYC. The brand's trajectory is moving from external joy → private ritual → intimate self-care.
- **New album signals return:** *Kiss All the Time. Disco, Occasionally.* drops March 6, 2026. The title alone is maximalist surrealism — punctuation as personality. First single "Aperture" performed live at the 2026 BRIT Awards after a 3-year red carpet absence.
- **Fashion evolution:** BRIT Awards look was oversized Chanel pinstripe with a mint shirt — a shift from the Gucci-era flamboyance toward something more structured but still fluid. The maximalism is maturing.

### Macro Winds
- **"Past Reveals Future"** is the dominant curatorial frame — nostalgia not as retreat but as raw material for transformation. Vintage forms made uncanny.
- **The physical world leans human:** As digital spaces get cleaner and AI-generated, physical objects are swinging hard toward handmade marks, tactile imperfection, analog warmth.
- **Surrealism goes domestic:** No longer confined to gallery objects — surrealist logic is entering tableware, candle design, furniture, and textiles at commercial scale.

### Tension Worth Exploiting
Harry Styles' Pleasing brand is moving from public joy to private ritual at the exact moment the design world is swinging from sterile minimalism to surreal, tactile maximalism — creating a collision between **"intimate domestic ceremony"** and **"objects that feel alive and in transformation."**

## Phase 2: The "Edit" Scan

### Aesthetic Movement: 【Domestic Surrealism (生活超現実主義)】

**Voo Store Filter — Raw × Refined:**
Styles reads as "pure refined" on the surface — Gucci campaigns, polished pop stardom. But beneath that surface is something genuinely raw: the gender fluidity that still provokes, the sexual wellness pivot that's confrontational, the 70s maximalism that's inherently unruly. For home objects, this tension means: vessels that are clearly touched by human hands — drip marks left intact, glaze pooling in valleys — but existing in spaces of considered composition. The product should feel like it was *found* in a flea market in Provence and then placed on a shelf in a London townhouse with absolute certainty.

**TSUTAYA Filter — Lifestyle Proposal:**
These are not "candles" or "plates." They are a proposal for how to inhabit domestic space as a form of self-expression. *Harry's House* already coded home-as-identity. The TSUTAYA organizational logic here is not by category but by **emotional scenario** — objects grouped by mood, not function. A candle next to a linen napkin next to a ceramic vessel, because they all belong to "The Dinner You Cook Alone on Purpose."

**Indie Print Filter — Analog Appreciation:**
Styles' maximalism is inherently analog — vinyl records, hand-embroidered suits, rings stacked imperfectly on every finger. This brand should feel like a hand-printed Risograph zine of domestic life: color registration slightly off, paper stock you can feel under your thumb, photography that looks like someone's actual kitchen at 2 AM rather than a styled set. Typography references 70s psychedelic poster art but processed through a contemporary indie press.

### Visual Mood
*A photograph that could exist in the art pages of Numéro: A kitchen counter in late-afternoon light, honey-gold and slightly overexposed. A ceramic vessel shaped like a soft, almost-melting form holds a candle — the wax has dripped down one side, hardening into a new geography. Behind it, a linen napkin in an impossible shade of lilac, creased but never folded. A single fig on a hand-thrown plate that is slightly oval, not round. A ring has been left beside the plate, catching light. Nothing matches. Everything belongs.*

## Phase 3: The Blueprint

### 1. Brand Name & Core Logic

- **Brand Name:** **FONDRE**
- **Core Statement:** *"Objects that remember being something else."*
- **Brand Logic:** *Fondre* is French for "to melt" — as in wax, as in boundaries, as in the dissolution of the rigid into the soft. Harry Styles is the figure who made fluidity a way of living, not a marketing position. FONDRE extends that fluidity into domestic space: every object exists in a state of transformation. Candles that melt into sculptural forms. Ceramics that look mid-metamorphosis. Linens that drape like fabric dreaming of being water. The brand connects Styles' maximalist surrealism to the Maison & Objet 2026 "Metamorphosis" current, filtered through the analog warmth of Global Indie living. It is not a home goods line — it is a *domestic practice*.

### 2. Hero Product

**"Séance" — 4-Wick Sculptural Candle in Hand-Thrown Stoneware Vessel**

**Vessel:**
Hand-thrown stoneware, produced by a rotating roster of independent ceramicists (each production run credited by name — *the Archive logic*). Every vessel is intentionally asymmetrical: one wall thicker than the other, glaze pooling in unpredictable valleys, the base bearing the visible thumbprint of the maker. Surface texture alternates between matte and glossy where the glaze broke during kiln firing — a record of heat and chance.

**Wax:**
A proprietary blend of rapeseed and coconut wax, hand-poured in chromatic layers so each burn reveals a new color beneath — lilac gives way to terracotta gives way to cream. The wax is engineered to melt *over* the vessel's edge, cascading down and hardening into an evolving topography. After the candle is spent, the vessel and its frozen wax rivers become a permanent sculptural object. No two are alike. The destruction *is* the design.

**Scent — "Pressed Flower Library":**
Opening note: torn fig leaf and warm linen, green and textile.
Heart: beeswax and crushed violet stem — not sweet, almost vegetal.
Base: aged oak and a ghost of tobacco, like opening a drawer in a house you've never visited but somehow recognize.

**Packaging:**
Wrapped in unbleached cotton muslin (reusable as a napkin — *nothing is single-use*), tied with waxed cotton cord. The label is letterpress-printed on handmade paper with visible fiber inclusions, type set in a hand-drawn serif that references 1970s psychedelic poster art — slightly off-registration, as if the ink is still drying. On the reverse, a single line in sans-serif: *"This object will not look the same tomorrow."*

### 3. Core Ritual / Scenario

**【The Dinner for One, Set for Two】**

FONDRE's central ritual is the meal you cook alone — not from loneliness, but from devotion to the act itself. You set the table with two FONDRE plates: one for eating, one for the bread you tore by hand. You light the Séance candle not for ambiance but to *watch it transform* — the wax finding its path over the vessel's lip, building a new form while you eat. The linen napkin is draped across the table's edge, never folded into a shape. The music is playing but you've stopped noticing — the room itself has become the experience.

This is domestic surrealism: the everyday made strange and beautiful through intention. It is not "self-care" (a word FONDRE never uses). It is the practice of treating your own domestic life as an artwork in progress.

**Time:** Evening. After everything else is done.
**Sound:** Whatever was playing when you forgot to pay attention.
**Duration:** However long the wax takes to reach the table.

### 4. Market Gap

**Current Landscape:**
Celebrity home goods exist in two exhausted modes. Mode A: the mass-market tasteful neutral — Goop candles at $75 that smell "nice" and disappear into any room, Gwyneth's empire of curated beige. Mode B: the ironic hypebeast object — Supreme accessories, MSCHF conceptual furniture, things you buy to photograph and never use. The serious end of artisanal home objects (Faye Toogood, Eny Lee Parker, Casa Muñoz) has zero celebrity bridge — it speaks to the design world exclusively.

Meanwhile, Harry Styles' own Pleasing brand has pivoted to sexual wellness, leaving the "domestic joy" territory he explored with *Harry's House* entirely unoccupied. The album was a cultural signal that millions received — home as identity, home as creative space — and no product followed it into that territory.

**The Gap:**
No one is making surrealist, gender-fluid home objects — ceramics and candles and linens that reject the "neutral palette + clean lines" orthodoxy of the Aesop/Muji consensus WITHOUT collapsing into kitsch or irony. FONDRE occupies the space between Fornasetti (surreal but untouchable, a museum proposition) and Urban Outfitters (surreal but disposable, a trend-cycle casualty). It is surrealism you *actually use*, that changes as you use it, that bears the marks of your life on its surfaces.

**Why Harry Styles:**
He is the only living celebrity who has simultaneously earned credibility in high fashion (Gucci, Chanel), indie culture (Pleasing's early zine-like energy, the residency tour model), and mass emotional intimacy (a fan base that treats his work as personal ritual). His gender fluidity is not a marketing angle — it is the *reason* these objects reject gendered design language. And his album title already declared the thesis: the house is the creative project.

### 5. Magazine Spread (*Numéro*)

**Target Publication:** *Numéro* — art editorial pages, not commercial fashion.

**Photography Direction:** Shot by Harley Weir or Tyler Mitchell. Conceptual domestic intimacy — every image feels like walking into someone's private evening, uninvited but welcomed.

**Left Page:**
A domestic still life, almost Baroque in density but contemporary in light. A FONDRE Séance candle mid-burn on a vintage marble side table, its wax rivers frozen mid-cascade down one carved leg. Behind it, a loose stack of FONDRE linen napkins in mismatched surrealist pastels — lilac, celadon, a pink that's almost the color of skin. The light is late-afternoon, honey-gold, pouring from a window outside the frame. A single hand enters from the left edge — rings on every finger, nails painted an imperfect lavender — reaching for a ceramic cup that is slightly too large, slightly too organic in form, to be merely functional.

**Right Page:**
Close-up of the Séance vessel, post-burn. The candle is spent. The wax has formed an unrepeatable landscape around the stoneware — frozen rivers, miniature cliffs, pools of solidified lilac and terracotta. It is no longer a candle. It is an artifact of an evening that happened.

Type runs vertically along the right margin in a hand-drawn serif:
*"Every evening is a séance with who you were this morning."*

Below, smaller, in clean sans-serif tracking wide:
**FONDRE** — Objects in Transformation.

**Overall Direction:** Numéro's art-editorial register — conceptual, slightly unsettling in its beauty. Nothing is staged to look "livable." Everything is staged to look *felt*.

### 6. Retail Space Concept

**Location Archetype:** A former florist's shop in London's Marylebone (primary), or a ground-floor space in Shimokitazawa, Tokyo — chosen for the neighborhood's secondhand/indie energy over Daikanyama's curated polish.

**Space — "The Living Room That Isn't Yours":**
Walls are washed in diluted terracotta pigment, applied unevenly by hand — deliberately imperfect, like a watercolor that stopped drying mid-stroke. The floor is original tile, unrestored. Shelving is a mix of reclaimed oak planks and bent brass rods — nothing is level, everything is intentional.

Products are NOT organized by category. Candles sit next to linens next to ceramics, arranged by **mood vignettes** — the TSUTAYA proposal logic applied to domestic objects. One shelf is "The Morning After a Good Conversation." Another is "The Afternoon You Cancelled Everything." Visitors understand the brand by navigating emotional states, not product SKUs.

In the center of the space: a table permanently set as **"The Dinner for One, Set for Two."** Two FONDRE plates, two linen napkins in clashing pastels, a Séance candle always burning. The wax accumulates over the life of the residency — by the final week, the candle and table have merged into a single sculptural object. Visitors are invited to sit. No one is asked to buy.

**Signature Detail:**
A wall-mounted **"Melt Clock"** — a large ceramic clock face where the numerals have been hand-sculpted to appear softening and dripping (the Dalí reference made domestic and useful). The clock runs. It is set to the wrong time on purpose — the hour of your last good meal alone, whatever that was.

**Scent:** The space is fragranced with "Pressed Flower Library" at all times — low diffusion, never overpowering. Visitors smell it on their coat when they leave.
