Harry Styles is the rare figure who has made fluidity itself an aesthetic position — not just gender fluidity in fashion (the Gucci gowns, the pearl necklaces, the painted nails), but a fluidity of category. He moves between pop and psychedelia, between stadium spectacle and intimate residency, between Chanel pinstripe and vintage market finds. His visual language is rooted in 1970s maximalism — saturated color, clashing pattern, furniture-scale jewelry — but filtered through a contemporary surrealism that makes the familiar feel slightly dreamed.

Joy as subversion. Softness as strength. The domestic as creative canvas. His album Harry’s House was not a metaphor — it was a declaration that the interior life (literally: interiors) is the artistic frontier.

Domestic Surrealism  【生活超現実主義】

Voo Store Filter — Raw × Refined

Styles reads as “pure refined” on the surface — Gucci campaigns, polished pop stardom. But beneath it: the gender fluidity that still provokes, the sexual wellness pivot, the 70s maximalism that is inherently unruly. For home objects this means: vessels clearly touched by human hands — drip marks left intact, glaze pooling in valleys — existing in spaces of considered composition. Found in a Provence flea market, placed on a London shelf with absolute certainty.

TSUTAYA Filter — Lifestyle Proposal

These are not “candles” or “plates.” They are a proposal for how to inhabit domestic space as a form of self-expression. Harry’s House already coded home-as-identity. The organizational logic is not by category but by emotional scenario — objects grouped by mood, not function. A candle next to a linen next to a ceramic vessel, because they all belong to “The Dinner You Cook Alone on Purpose.”

Indie Print Filter — Analog Appreciation

Styles’ maximalism is inherently analog — vinyl records, hand-embroidered suits, rings stacked imperfectly. This brand should feel like a hand-printed Risograph zine of domestic life: color registration slightly off, paper stock you can feel, photography that looks like someone’s actual kitchen at 2 AM. Typography references 70s psychedelic poster art, processed through a contemporary indie press.

Phase 3 — The Blueprint

Brand Concept

01
Brand Name & Core Logic
The Foundation

FONDRE

French: “to melt” — as in wax, as in boundaries, as in the dissolution of the rigid into the soft

“Objects that remember being something else.”

Harry Styles is the figure who made fluidity a way of living, not a marketing position. FONDRE extends that fluidity into domestic space: every object exists in a state of transformation. Candles that melt into sculptural forms. Ceramics that look mid-metamorphosis. Linens that drape like fabric dreaming of being water. The brand connects Styles’ maximalist surrealism to the Maison & Objet 2026 “Metamorphosis” current, filtered through the analog warmth of Global Indie living. It is not a home goods line — it is a domestic practice.

02
Hero Product
“Séance” — 4-Wick Sculptural Candle in Hand-Thrown Stoneware

Vessel

Hand-thrown stoneware by a rotating roster of independent ceramicists, each production run credited by name. Every vessel is intentionally asymmetrical: one wall thicker than the other, glaze pooling in unpredictable valleys, the base bearing the visible thumbprint of the maker. Surface texture alternates between matte and glossy where the glaze broke during kiln firing — a record of heat and chance.

Wax

A proprietary blend of rapeseed and coconut wax, hand-poured in chromatic layers — lilac gives way to terracotta gives way to cream. The wax is engineered to melt over the vessel’s edge, cascading down and hardening into an evolving topography. After the candle is spent, the vessel and its frozen wax rivers become a permanent sculptural object. No two are alike. The destruction is the design.

Packaging

Wrapped in unbleached cotton muslin (reusable as a napkin — nothing is single-use), tied with waxed cotton cord. The label is letterpress-printed on handmade paper with visible fiber inclusions, type in a hand-drawn serif referencing 1970s psychedelic poster art — slightly off-registration, as if the ink is still drying. On the reverse: “This object will not look the same tomorrow.”

Design Philosophy

The Séance candle is not consumed — it is transformed. Each burn is an act of collaboration between the user and the object. The wax rivers record the evenings they witnessed: how long you sat, which direction the air moved, whether you were alone. It is a diary written in rapeseed and stoneware.

“Pressed Flower Library”

Opening

Torn fig leaf and warm linen — green and textile, like pressing your face into a sunlit curtain in a room full of plants.

Heart

Beeswax and crushed violet stem — not sweet, almost vegetal. The smell of something growing in a room where someone has been reading.

Base

Aged oak and a ghost of tobacco — like opening a drawer in a house you’ve never visited but somehow recognize.

03
Core Ritual
The Behavioral Catalyst

【 The Dinner for One, Set for Two 】

FONDRE’s central ritual is the meal you cook alone — not from loneliness, but from devotion to the act itself. You set the table with two FONDRE plates: one for eating, one for the bread you tore by hand. You light the Séance candle not for ambiance but to watch it transform — the wax finding its path over the vessel’s lip, building a new form while you eat. The linen napkin is draped across the table’s edge, never folded into a shape. The music is playing but you’ve stopped noticing — the room itself has become the experience.

This is domestic surrealism: the everyday made strange and beautiful through intention. It is not “self-care” (a word FONDRE never uses). It is the practice of treating your own domestic life as an artwork in progress.

Time

Evening. After everything else is done.

Sound

Whatever was playing when you forgot to pay attention.

Duration

However long the wax takes to reach the table.

04
Market Gap
The Unoccupied Niche

Celebrity home goods exist in two exhausted modes. Mode A: the mass-market tasteful neutral — candles that smell “nice” and disappear into any room, empires of curated beige. Mode B: the ironic hypebeast object — conceptual furniture, things you buy to photograph and never use. The serious end of artisanal home objects has zero celebrity bridge — it speaks to the design world exclusively.

Meanwhile, Styles’ own Pleasing brand has pivoted to sexual wellness, leaving the “domestic joy” territory he explored with Harry’s House entirely unoccupied. The album was a cultural signal that millions received — home as identity, home as creative space — and no product followed it into that territory.

FONDRE Objects in Transformation

Overall Direction

Numéro’s art-editorial register — conceptual, slightly unsettling in its beauty. Nothing is staged to look “livable.” Everything is staged to look felt.

06
Retail Space Concept
“The Living Room That Isn’t Yours”

Location: A former florist’s shop in London’s Marylebone, or a ground-floor space in Shimokitazawa, Tokyo — chosen for the neighborhood’s secondhand/indie energy over Daikanyama’s curated polish.

Space & Materials

Walls washed in diluted terracotta pigment, applied unevenly by hand — deliberately imperfect, like a watercolor that stopped drying mid-stroke. The floor is original tile, unrestored. Shelving is a mix of reclaimed oak planks and bent brass rods — nothing is level, everything is intentional.

Organization Logic

Products are NOT organized by category. Candles sit next to linens next to ceramics, arranged by mood vignettes. One shelf is “The Morning After a Good Conversation.” Another is “The Afternoon You Cancelled Everything.” Visitors navigate emotional states, not product SKUs.

Central Installation

A table permanently set as “The Dinner for One, Set for Two.” Two plates, two linen napkins in clashing pastels, a Séance candle always burning. The wax accumulates over the residency’s life — by the final week, candle and table have merged into a single sculptural object. Visitors are invited to sit. No one is asked to buy.

Atmosphere

The space is fragranced with “Pressed Flower Library” at all times — low diffusion, never overpowering. Visitors smell it on their coat when they leave. The scent becomes the memory of the visit.

A wall-mounted “Melt Clock” — a large ceramic clock face where the numerals have been hand-sculpted to appear softening and dripping. The clock runs. It is set to the wrong time on purpose — the hour of your last good meal alone, whatever that was.
