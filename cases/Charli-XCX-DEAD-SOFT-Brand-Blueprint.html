Charli XCX is a figure built on productive friction — the hyperpop pioneer who wins Wallpaper* Design Awards, the illegal rave veteran who campaigns for Saint Laurent, the pop star who makes an A24 film deconstructing pop stardom. She owns two homes that are her aesthetic DNA: a minimal Hackney flat (Ligne Roset Togo, stacks of books, white walls) and a maximalist LA compound (black-on-black kitchen, glass walls, 18ft beamed ceiling). She lives between excess and restraint, and her body knows the difference.

Authenticity for her is emotional, not factual. “I don’t care if they tell the truth or lie or play a character or adopt a persona or fabricate entire scenarios and worlds. To me that’s the point, that’s the drama, that’s the fun, that’s the FANTASY.”

Voo Store — Raw × Refined

The tension lives in the collision between club-sweat energy and the softness of bedding. Industrial textures — raw-edge selvedge, visible weave structure, garment-dye irregularity — meet the most intimate domestic surface: the sheet you press your face into. Heavyweight construction, imperfect finishing, exposed thread ends. But the hand is pure luxury: enzyme-washed until all rigidity dissolves, a surface you could sleep on for twelve hours without a crease mark. The outside says screen-printed in a Kreuzberg studio. The inside says engineered for a body that earned its rest.

TSUTAYA — Lifestyle Proposal

This is not “bedding.” It is a proposal for how a body transitions. The Static Set is organized not by thread count or color name but by state: “Frequency Grey” is the standard crash, “Dawn Static” is the lighter variant for the body that will wake early, “Club Black” is for the body that wants total absorption. Each set ships in a drawstring muslin bag that doubles as a laundry bag or travel roll — because the person who buys DEAD SOFT crashes in different places.

Phase 3 — The Blueprint

Brand Concept

01
Brand Name & Core Logic
The Foundation

DEAD SOFT

Metallurgy: metal annealed to zero hardness — maximum pliability through maximum heat

“Your body has a frequency. This is what it sounds like at rest.”

In metallurgy, “dead soft” describes metal that has been fully annealed — heated to extreme temperatures and slowly cooled until all internal stress is released. Zero hardness. Maximum malleability. Charli XCX is an artist who has been through the fire: illegal raves at 16, hyperpop’s compressed intensity, a Brat era that became a cultural event, and an A24 film deconstructing her own fame. DEAD SOFT proposes that what comes after the performance — the crash, the horizontal surrender, the 4 AM stillness — deserves its own material world. Textiles for the body in its post-performance state: maximum softness earned through maximum intensity.

“The Static Set” — 3-Piece Bedding System

Weave

400GSM organic cotton percale, garment-dyed in small batches and enzyme-washed three times to achieve the “dead soft” hand — zero stiffness, maximum drape, the tactile equivalent of a t-shirt that has been through 200 wash cycles. Matte, almost chalky surface quality. No sheen. No polish.

Hand

Pick up the pillowcase and it drapes over your hand like a second skin — there is weight but zero rigidity. The enzyme wash has broken every fiber until the fabric moves like liquid. Dense enough to feel substantial against the face, breathable enough for a body still cooling down from six hours of movement.

Selvedge Detail

A single acid-green thread (Pantone 3570 C) runs through the selvedge edge — a private signature. Almost invisible when the sheet is on the bed. You only see it when you fold it, pull back the corner, or get close enough to touch. The residue of a former life as a neon-green Brat-era artifact, now washed into something quieter.

Packaging

Ships in a screen-printed muslin drawstring bag — not a box. The bag doubles as a laundry bag or travel roll. Blurred Arial in acid green on raw muslin. Inside: an 8-page Riso-printed zine replaces the care card. Material story, washing philosophy, and one photograph of an unmade bed shot by someone who understands that crumpled is more beautiful than crisp.

03
Core Ritual
The Behavioral Catalyst

【 The 4 AM Horizontal 】

You have been vertical for six hours — or eight, or twelve. Dancing, performing, working, being ON in whatever way your life demands. Your ears are still ringing. Your skin is cooling. The phone is on 4%. You don’t brush your teeth. You don’t set an alarm.

You peel off whatever you wore and fall — not lie, fall — onto a surface that absorbs you. Not into “luxury sleep” but into the specific relief of a body that earned its rest. The Static Set is designed for this threshold moment: heavyweight enough to feel like being held, soft enough to feel like dissolving. The flat sheet settles over you with the weight of permission. The product does not optimize sleep. It does not track REM cycles. It serves one moment: the crash. Everything after is your business.

Time

4:00 AM — or whenever vertical becomes impossible.

Sound

Whatever is still playing on the phone on the nightstand, volume at 3.

Duration

Until the light changes.

04
Market Gap
The Unoccupied Niche

Position A: The Heirloom Set — Frette, Matouk, Sferra. Beautiful linens sold through heritage, thread count, and aspirational domesticity. They assume the bed is a sanctuary for gentle people with gentle lives. The photography shows a bed no one has ever slept in. Position B: The Volume Comfort Set — IKEA, H&M Home, Target Casaluna. Commodity comfort at scale. Adequate material, zero cultural address, no point of view. Position C: No bedding brand speaks to the body that lives hard and crashes harder — the clubber, the performer, the nightshift creative, the touring musician. The “after party” aesthetic exists in fashion but has zero domestic object equivalent.

06
Retail Space Concept
“The Former Laundry”

Location: A former industrial laundry in Kreuzberg, Berlin — the irony is the point. Poured concrete floors with original drain channels still visible. A bedding brand born in the architecture of cleaning.

Space & Materials

Ceiling-mounted industrial strip lighting on dimmers — the space shifts from bright clinical (like a laundromat at noon) to warm amber (like a bedroom at 4 AM) over the course of each day. One wall: floor-to-ceiling raw steel shelving. Each Static Set folded and wrapped in its muslin bag, hanging from butcher’s hooks. Industrial hanging that makes fabric look like equipment, not decoration.

The Display Bed

Opposite wall: a single full-size bed, perfectly made each morning, then deliberately unmade by hand before the store opens. Sheets pulled, pillow dented, one corner trailing to the floor. The bed is displayed in its crashed state. Visitors are invited to lie down. A small sign in Space Mono reads: “EVALUATE BY TOUCH. NO MIRRORS.”

Sound

A continuous low-frequency ambient drone — the sonic equivalent of the hum in a room after the last guest has left. Designed to sit just below conscious awareness. Not silence. Not music. The residue between them.

Fitting Rooms

The fitting room is a bed, not a changing room. Enclosed by floor-to-ceiling industrial curtains on a hospital track, each “fitting room” contains a single bed made with the Static Set. You pull the curtain, lie down, and decide if you’re buying based entirely on how the fabric feels against your skin at rest. No visual evaluation. No mirrors.

The product is assessed through the body’s only honest surface: the face pressed into a pillowcase. The store has no mirrors because DEAD SOFT is not evaluated by how it looks. It is evaluated by how it feels against the part of you that was just the most alive — your skin, still warm, still carrying the residue of wherever you just were. The Static Set is the surface that receives that residue without judgement.
